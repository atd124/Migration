NATIONAL SECURITY

Record arrests at the border (again)

Nine months into fiscal year 2022, US Border Patrol has already apprehended 1,653,770 million migrants, nearly as many as last year's record high.

By Anthony D'Arrigo

August 7, 2022

Pair of migrant families from Brazil pass through a gap in the border wall to reach the US in Yuma, Arizona on June 10, 2021. Eugene Garcia | AP file | NBC News





Arrests of migrants illegally crossing the Southwest border will surpass last year’s record of 1.682 million. According to the latest official statistics from the Department of Homeland Security, US Border Patrol has already apprehended 1,653,770 million migrants over the first nine months of fiscal year 2022. At this unprecedented rate, 2022 will almost certainly exceed 2 million arrests before the fiscal year ends in September.





For a sense of the magnitude of the problem and how quickly it changed, consider 2011 to 2018. Under two different administrations over that eight-year period, US Border Patrol averaged about 377,000 apprehensions a year. Now, they are averaging that many arrests every two months—a 6x increase.

But when we widen the scope to 2000, the overall trend looks different. Although last year was indeed a record, the number of arrests wasn't unprecedented. The number of apprehensions in 2000 were nearly identical to 2021. And back then, according to NPR, immigration authorities caught fewer migrants. By DHS's own estimate, there were nearly 4 million illegal crossings that year, and Border Patrol agents caught less than half.

And if we extend it even further, a full 40 years back to 1983, we see how that 8-year period between 2011 and 2018 is actually an outlier. From 1983 to 2006, 1.2 million apprehensions a year was normal.

Who was crossing the border was also very different back then. It was mostly single men from Mexico trying to evade capture by Border Patrol. The earliest DHS data on apprehensions by citizenship, for example, illustrates that in 2007, over 93% of all migrants arrested at the border were Mexican. Today, just over a third are from Mexico. A rising number, especially this year, are coming from South America, Africa, and the Caribbean.
